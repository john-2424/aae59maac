\begin{abstract}
We study reinforcement learning for maximizing the algebraic connectivity
$\lambda_2(L)$ of a weighted communication graph, the quantity that
governs the convergence rate of linear consensus in multi-agent
networks. We show that the bounded-weight, budget-constrained
reweighting problem is equivalent to a convex semidefinite program
(SDP) of Boyd and Ghosh, and we use the SDP optimum as a quantitative
upper bound against which to measure a learned policy. A proximal
policy optimization (PPO) agent trained on Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi supports
with $n=20$ attains $87.3\%$ of the SDP optimum on a representative
held-out graph and a mean $\lambda_2 = 0.795 \pm 0.146$ across
twenty-five held-out graphs, a $1.31\times$ lift over the uniform
baseline. The same policy class, trained under stochastic edge
failures, continues to dominate classical baselines at every failure
rate we tested, and in a geometric swarm regime (where the SDP bound
does not apply) produces spread-preserving motion that outperforms a
collision-avoiding centroid heuristic. A discrete-rewiring variant
fails to generalize across topologies with a flat
action-space policy, which we analyze and report as a motivating
limitation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}
\label{sec:intro}
Distributed consensus is a foundational primitive in multi-agent
systems, sensor fusion, swarm coordination, decentralized estimation,
and distributed optimization. In a standard consensus process, agents
iteratively exchange information with local neighbors and
asymptotically agree on a shared quantity. The speed of this agreement
depends strongly on the communication graph. For undirected graphs,
the second-smallest eigenvalue of the graph Laplacian, denoted
$\lambda_2(L)$, is the algebraic connectivity or spectral gap, and
plays a central role in characterizing connectivity and convergence
speed \cite{Fiedler1973,OlfatiSaber2007,RenBeard2005}. A larger
spectral gap generally implies faster information mixing and faster
decay of disagreement~\cite{Olshevsky2009}.

This work studies whether reinforcement learning (RL) can improve
consensus performance by optimizing the edge weights or topology of
the communication graph under realistic constraints. Network design is
rarely unconstrained in practice: real systems face limited
communication budgets, bounded link weights, degree caps, intermittent
connectivity, and geometric constraints due to agent motion or sensing
range. In the restricted setting of a fixed connected support with
bounded nonnegative weights and a total-weight budget, the problem of
maximizing $\lambda_2$ admits a convex semidefinite formulation
developed by Boyd and co-authors
\cite{Boyd2006,GhoshBoyd2006,XiaoBoyd2004}, known as the Fastest
Distributed Linear Averaging (FDLA) relaxation, which is solvable to
global optimality by interior-point
methods~\cite{BoydVandenberghe2004}. This setting is therefore a
useful analytic anchor: it tells us the best achievable $\lambda_2$
that any policy can attain.

The analytic picture breaks down in three regimes of practical
interest. First, under stochastic edge failures the Laplacian becomes
a random matrix at every step, and the SDP optimum on the nominal
support is no longer attainable in expectation. Second, when the
support itself is a decision variable, maximizing algebraic
connectivity over topologies is NP-hard~\cite{Mosk2006}, so convex
methods give no tight bound. Third, when the graph support is induced
by agent positions---as in random geometric graphs arising from
proximity-based communication in robotic swarms---the map from
decision variables (positions) to $\lambda_2$ is neither linear nor
convex, and related classical connectivity-maintenance results are
largely local~\cite{SabattiniEtAl2013,YangFreeman2010,KimMesbahi2006}.

These three regimes motivate the main question of this work:
\emph{can a single learned policy class approximate the convex
optimum where it applies, and generalize to the regimes where it does
not?} We formulate the reweighting task as a constrained Markov
decision process~\cite{SuttonBarto2018} and train with proximal policy
optimization (PPO)~\cite{Schulman2017,SchulmanGAE2016}, treating the
FDLA SDP as both a theoretical anchor and a benchmark. We then test
the same policy class on discrete rewiring, perturbation-in-the-loop
training, and a geometric swarm extension. The broader motivation is
to situate learning-based topology control within the spectral
graph-theoretic framework~\cite{MesbahiEgerstedt2010}: RL is used not
as a replacement for graph theory but as an adaptive optimizer on top
of it. This work is part of a broader trend in graph reinforcement
learning for combinatorial design~\cite{Darvariu2024,Darvariu2021,Dai2017}.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
Consider a network of $n$ agents represented by an undirected graph
$G_k = (V, E_k, W_k)$ with $|V| = n$, where $E_k$ is the edge set at
time step $k$ and $W_k \in \R^{n \times n}$ is a symmetric nonnegative
weight matrix. Let $D_k = \diag(W_k \mathbf{1})$ be the degree matrix
and let the graph Laplacian be
\begin{equation}
L_k = D_k - W_k.
\end{equation}
Each agent $i$ holds a scalar state $x_i(k)$, and the network state is
$x(k) \in \R^n$. The discrete-time consensus dynamics are
\begin{equation}
x(k+1) = x(k) - \alpha\, L_k\, x(k),
\label{eq:consensus}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha > 0$ is a step size chosen so that the update is stable,
e.g.\ $\alpha < 2/\lambda_{\max}(L_k)$. The disagreement vector is
$e(k) = x(k) - \bar{x}(k)\mathbf{1}$ with
$\bar{x}(k) = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}^\top x(k)$, and we evaluate
convergence through the disagreement energy
$J_{\text{dis}}(k) = \|e(k)\|_2^2$ and the $\varepsilon$-settling time
$\tau_\varepsilon = \min\{k : J_{\text{dis}}(k)/J_{\text{dis}}(0) \le \varepsilon\}$.
For connected undirected graphs, $J_{\text{dis}}(k)$ decays
geometrically at a rate governed by $\lambda_2(L_k)$, which motivates
the spectral-gap objective~\cite{Fiedler1973,OlfatiSaber2007}.

\subsection{Constrained reweighting}
We parameterize $W$ by an edge-weight vector $w \in \R^m_{\ge 0}$,
where $m = |E|$. Given endpoints $e_k = (i_k, j_k)$ and incidence
vectors $a_k = \mathbf{e}_{i_k} - \mathbf{e}_{j_k}$, the Laplacian is
\begin{equation}
L(w) = \sum_{k=1}^{m} w_k\, a_k a_k^\top.
\label{eq:Lw-top}
\end{equation}
The admissible weight set is the polyhedral convex set
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W} = \Big\{ w \in \R^m \;:\; 0 \le w_k \le w_{\max},\;
\textstyle\sum_{k=1}^m w_k \le B \Big\},
\label{eq:Wset-top}
\end{equation}
where $w_{\max}$ is the per-edge bound and $B$ is a total
communication budget.

\begin{problem}[Constrained spectral-gap maximization]
\label{prob:lambda2}
Given a fixed connected support $E$ with $m$ edges and parameters
$(w_{\max}, B)$, find
\begin{equation}
\lambda_2^\star \;:=\; \max_{w \in \mathcal{W}} \; \lambda_2(L(w)).
\label{eq:primal-top}
\end{equation}
\end{problem}

\subsection{Markov decision process}
We cast Problem~\ref{prob:lambda2} as a finite-horizon MDP
$(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma)$ in which the policy proposes
reweightings on top of a warm start.

\begin{definition}[Reweighting MDP]
\label{def:mdp}
At step $k$, the observation
$s_k \in \mathcal{S}$ concatenates: (i) degree statistics (mean,
standard deviation, max, min) of $L(w_k)$; (ii) the top-$\kappa$
Laplacian eigenvalues with $\kappa{=}4$; (iii) the current weight
vector $w_k$ padded to $m_{\max}$; (iv) the normalized remaining
budget; and (v) an exponential moving average of $\log J_{\text{dis}}$.
The action $a_k \in [-1,1]^m$ is mapped to weights by
$w_{k+1} = \Pi_\mathcal{W}\!\big(\tfrac{w_{\max}}{2}(a_k + 1)\big)$,
where $\Pi_\mathcal{W}$ rescales by $B / \sum w$ if the budget
constraint is violated. The stage reward is
\begin{equation}
r_k = \lambda_2(L(w_{k+1})) - \beta\,\mathrm{Cost}(w_{k+1}) - \gamma\,\mathrm{Violations}(w_{k+1}),
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{Cost}(w) = \sum_k w_k$ penalizing budget use and
$\mathrm{Violations}(w)$ a hinge loss on bound violations before
projection.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Modeling scope]
\label{rem:scope}
Definition~\ref{def:mdp} assumes the policy observes spectral
statistics of the \emph{global} Laplacian. This is a deliberate
simplification that isolates the learning question from the
decentralization question. A fully distributed variant that replaces
(ii) with a localized proxy (e.g.\ a distributed Fiedler vector
estimate \cite{YangFreeman2010,SabattiniEtAl2013}) is left to future
work; we address this trade-off explicitly in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.
\end{remark}

Discrete topology control and geometric motion control are formalized
as variants of Definition~\ref{def:mdp} with modified action spaces;
we introduce those in Section~\ref{sec:main-extensions} only after
developing the analytic anchor.

\section{Main Results}
\label{sec:main}

\subsection*{Key Idea}
\label{sec:key-idea}
We frame algebraic-connectivity maximization under bounded edge
weights and a communication budget as a constrained Markov decision
process whose optimum is upper-bounded by a convex semidefinite
program, namely the Fastest Distributed Linear Averaging (FDLA)
relaxation of Boyd and
co-authors \cite{XiaoBoyd2004,Boyd2006,GhoshBoyd2006}. This bound
reframes an otherwise heuristic benchmarking exercise as a
quantitative suboptimality claim: for any feasible reweighting policy
$\pi$ acting on a fixed support,
$\lambda_2(L(w_\pi)) \le s^\star$, where $s^\star$ is the SDP optimum.
We then show that a Proximal Policy Optimization
\cite{Schulman2017} agent observing only graph features and consensus
statistics learns a policy that attains
$\lambda_2(L(w_\pi))/s^\star \approx 0.87$ on a representative
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi support of size $n{=}20$ with tight budget $B{=}25$.
Because the FDLA SDP ceases to apply when the underlying edge set is
itself a decision variable (discrete rewiring) or when the graph
support is induced by agent positions (random geometric graphs), we
further evaluate the same policy class in those two settings and
report its generalization to regimes where no convex upper bound is
available.

\subsection{Convex upper bound on achievable algebraic connectivity}
\label{sec:sdp}
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a fixed undirected connected graph on $n$ nodes.
Write $E = \{e_1, \ldots, e_m\}$ with endpoints $e_k = (i_k, j_k)$,
and let $a_k = \mathbf{e}_{i_k} - \mathbf{e}_{j_k} \in \R^n$ where
$\mathbf{e}_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector. For a nonnegative
weight vector $w \in \R^m_{\ge 0}$, the weighted graph Laplacian is
\begin{equation}
L(w) \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{m} w_k\, a_k a_k^\top
\;=\; A\,\diag(w)\,A^\top,
\label{eq:Lw}
\end{equation}
with $A = [a_1, \ldots, a_m] \in \R^{n \times m}$ the signed
incidence matrix. By construction $L(w)$ is symmetric positive
semidefinite and $L(w)\mathbf{1} = 0$, so the smallest eigenvalue of
$L(w)$ is $0$ with eigenvector $\mathbf{1}$, and the algebraic
connectivity is the second smallest eigenvalue
\cite{Fiedler1973,OlfatiSaber2007}. By the Courant--Fischer
characterization \cite{HornJohnson2013},
\begin{equation}
\lambda_2(L(w)) \;=\; \min_{\substack{v \in \R^n \\ v \perp \mathbf{1},\; \|v\|=1}} v^\top L(w)\, v.
\label{eq:rayleigh}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}[Boyd \cite{Boyd2006}; Ghosh and Boyd \cite{GhoshBoyd2006}]
\label{thm:fdla}
Problem~\ref{prob:lambda2} is equivalent to the semidefinite program
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
s^\star \;=\; \max_{w,\,s} \quad & s \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & L(w) \;\succeq\; s\!\left(I_n - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top\right), \\
& w \in \mathcal{W},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:sdp}
\end{equation}
in the sense that $\lambda_2^\star = s^\star$, and any optimizer
$w^\star$ of \eqref{eq:sdp} is a maximizer of \eqref{eq:primal-top}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\Pi := I_n - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ be the
orthogonal projector onto $\mathbf{1}^\perp$. Since $L(w) \succeq 0$
and $L(w)\mathbf{1} = 0$, the nontrivial eigenvalues of $L(w)$ are
exactly the eigenvalues of $\Pi L(w) \Pi$ restricted to
$\mathbf{1}^\perp$. Equation~\eqref{eq:rayleigh} therefore gives
\[
\lambda_2(L(w)) \;=\; \sup\{\, s \in \R : v^\top L(w) v \ge s\, v^\top \Pi v
\;\; \forall\, v \in \R^n \,\}.
\]
The universal inequality inside the supremum is a linear matrix
inequality $L(w) - s\Pi \succeq 0$, so
\[
\lambda_2(L(w)) \;=\; \sup\{\, s : L(w) - s\,\Pi \succeq 0 \,\}.
\]
Taking the supremum jointly over $w \in \mathcal{W}$ and $s$ yields
\eqref{eq:sdp}. Because $L(w)$ is linear in $w$ (cf.~\eqref{eq:Lw})
and $\mathcal{W}$ is convex, the program \eqref{eq:sdp} is a convex
SDP and is solvable to global optimality by interior-point methods
\cite{BoydVandenberghe2004,XiaoBoyd2004}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:fdla} plays two roles in this paper. First, it gives
a \emph{tight}, polynomial-time-computable upper bound on any
reweighting policy, learned or otherwise: no feasible
$w \in \mathcal{W}$ attains $\lambda_2(L(w)) > s^\star$. Second, it
defines the \emph{suboptimality ratio}
\begin{equation}
\rho_\pi \;:=\; \frac{\lambda_2\!\big(L(w_\pi)\big)}{s^\star}
\;\in\; [0, 1]
\label{eq:subopt}
\end{equation}
for any policy $\pi$ producing a feasible $w_\pi$. We evaluate our
PPO policy against \eqref{eq:subopt} in Section~\ref{sec:sim-m2} and
report $\rho_\pi \approx 0.87$ on an Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi support of
size $n = 20$, turning the comparison against classical heuristic
baselines (uniform, Metropolis--Hastings \cite{XiaoBoyd2004,Boyd2004})
into a quantitative optimality claim rather than a relative one.

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:fdla} assumes a fixed edge support $E$. When the
support is itself a decision variable, as in the discrete rewiring
setting of Section~\ref{sec:sim-m3a}, the feasible set
$\{L(w) : w \in \mathcal{W}\}$ is no longer convex and \eqref{eq:sdp}
ceases to provide a valid upper bound; maximizing $\lambda_2$ over
topologies is NP-hard in general \cite{Mosk2006}. Similarly, when $E$
is induced by agent positions as in the geometric swarm setting of
Section~\ref{sec:sim-m3c}, the map from positions to $L(w)$ is
nonlinear and nonconvex, and the SDP does not apply. This is
precisely the regime in which a learned policy earns its keep.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Learning algorithm}
\label{sec:algo}
The MDP of Definition~\ref{def:mdp} has a continuous action space, so
we train a stochastic Gaussian policy with proximal policy
optimization~\cite{Schulman2017}. The actor is a two-layer
multilayer perceptron with $\tanh$ activations and state-independent
log-standard-deviation; the critic shares the same backbone. Actions
are squashed through $\tanh$ and mapped into $[0, w_{\max}]^m$ before
being passed through the budget projection $\Pi_\mathcal{W}$. Generalized
advantage estimation~\cite{SchulmanGAE2016} with
$(\gamma, \lambda) = (0.99, 0.95)$ is used to compute advantages,
and the clipped surrogate objective with clip parameter $0.2$ is
optimized with Adam. Algorithm~\ref{alg:ppo} summarizes the training
loop.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{PPO for constrained $\lambda_2$ maximization}
\label{alg:ppo}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require actor $\pi_\theta$, critic $V_\phi$, graph family $\mathcal{G}$, bound $w_{\max}$, budget $B$, total frames $N$
\For{iteration $t = 1, 2, \ldots$}
  \State Sample a support $E \sim \mathcal{G}$; build incidence $A$
  \State Reset env: warm-start $w_0 \leftarrow$ Metropolis$(E)$, observe $s_0$
  \State Collect a rollout of $T$ transitions $\{(s_k, a_k, r_k, s_{k+1})\}$ under $\pi_\theta$
  \State Compute GAE targets $\{\hat A_k\}$ and value targets $\{\hat R_k\}$
  \For{each PPO epoch}
    \State Sample minibatches from the rollout buffer
    \State Update $\theta$ by $\nabla_\theta L^{\text{clip}}(\theta)$ with clip $\epsilon{=}0.2$
    \State Update $\phi$ by MSE on $V_\phi(s) \to \hat R$
  \EndFor
  \If{frames collected $\ge N$} \textbf{break}
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return $\pi_\theta$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Two practical choices are worth noting. First, we warm-start the
environment at the Metropolis weights rather than zero, so that the
reward at step zero is already nonzero and the critic is not forced
to learn from a flat baseline. Second, we keep a small entropy
coefficient ($0.005$) throughout training; without it the policy
collapsed back to the Metropolis warm start on Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi
supports in preliminary runs.

\subsection{Extensions beyond the convex regime}
\label{sec:main-extensions}
The SDP bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:fdla} is valid only when the edge
support $E$ is fixed and the mapping $w \mapsto L(w)$ is linear. Three
extensions of practical interest violate at least one of these
assumptions; the learning framework of Section~\ref{sec:algo} applies
to each with only local modifications, and they constitute the
remaining empirical contributions of this paper.

\emph{Discrete topology control.} Replacing the continuous
reweighting action by a categorical add/remove action over pairs
$(i,j)$ makes the feasible set of Laplacians non-convex, so the FDLA
SDP is no longer a valid upper bound. Maximizing $\lambda_2$ over
topologies is NP-hard~\cite{Mosk2006}, consistent with the broader
combinatorial-graph-RL literature~\cite{Darvariu2024,Darvariu2021,Dai2017}.

\emph{Stochastic edge failures.} Under i.i.d.\ Bernoulli edge
failures, the observed Laplacian at step $k$ is a random perturbation
of $L(w_k)$. A policy trained on clean supports has no explicit
robustness guarantee; training with perturbation-in-the-loop exposes
the policy to the stochastic regime during rollouts and is a natural
lever within the same PPO framework.

\emph{Geometric swarm control.} When agents carry positions
$p_i \in \R^2$ and edge weights are induced by a truncated Gaussian
kernel $W_{ij}(\mathbf{p}) = \exp(-(\|p_i-p_j\|/r)^2)$ with cutoff
$3r$, the map from $\mathbf{p}$ to $L$ is nonlinear and nonconvex, so
classical convex relaxations do not apply and decentralized connectivity-
maintenance controllers must be used as reference~\cite{SabattiniEtAl2013,YangFreeman2010,KimMesbahi2006}.
The same PPO pipeline with a velocity action space provides a learned
alternative.

\section{Simulations}
\label{sec:sim}

All experiments were implemented in PyTorch with the TorchRL PPO
implementation. Graphs, Laplacians, and consensus simulations use
NumPy and SciPy, and the FDLA SDP is solved with CVXPY and
SCS~\cite{BoydVandenberghe2004}.

\subsection{Baseline: topology, spectral gap, and convergence}
\label{sec:sim-m1}
We first verify the topology/$\lambda_2$/convergence chain on five
graph families (ring, grid, Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi, random geometric,
small-world) at sizes $n \in \{20, 50, 100\}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:m1-lambda2} plots $\lambda_2$ against edge count.
Dense random graphs (Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi, random geometric) achieve
$\lambda_2$ orders of magnitude larger than sparse structured graphs
(ring, small-world) at the same $n$, and the induced consensus
$\tau_\varepsilon$ varies accordingly---e.g.\ the $n{=}100$ ring
requires $\tau_\varepsilon = 500$ consensus steps to reach
$\varepsilon = 10^{-3}$ disagreement, whereas the $n{=}100$
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi graph reaches it in $7$ steps. The ring closed-form
$\lambda_2 = 2(1 - \cos(2\pi/n))$ and the complete-graph
$\lambda_2 = n$ are recovered to machine precision, confirming the
pipeline.

\subsection{PPO reweighting versus baselines versus the SDP bound}
\label{sec:sim-m2}
We train a single PPO policy on Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi supports with
$n = 20$, edge probability $p = 0.25$, bound $w_{\max} = 1.0$, and a
tight budget $B = 25$ (smaller than the expected sum of Metropolis
weights, so the policy must choose which edges to emphasize). Five
seeds are trained independently, and each is evaluated on five
held-out graphs drawn from the same distribution, yielding 25
independent held-out evaluations.

Figure~\ref{fig:m2-training} shows the training curve averaged over
five seeds, and Figure~\ref{fig:m2-eval} compares $\lambda_2$ and
$\tau_\varepsilon$ across policies on a representative seed. The
aggregate numbers are collected in Table~\ref{tab:m2}. The learned
policy attains a mean $\lambda_2 = 0.795 \pm 0.146$ versus
$0.607 \pm 0.119$ for uniform, $0.512 \pm 0.124$ for
degree-proportional, and $0.167 \pm 0.033$ for
Metropolis---a $1.31\times$ lift over the strongest baseline.

The most informative comparator is the FDLA SDP of
Theorem~\ref{thm:fdla}. On the seed-0 graph, CVXPY+SCS returns
$s^\star = 0.660$, and the PPO policy reaches
$\lambda_2 = 0.576$, i.e.\ $\rho_\pi = 87.3\%$ of the convex optimum.
This is the core quantitative claim of the paper. No feasible
policy---learned or analytic---can do better than the SDP, and PPO
closes $87\%$ of the gap between uniform reweighting and the provable
ceiling while consuming effectively the full budget
($24.99 / 25.00$). The residual $13\%$ is the approximation error of
a $2\times 128$ MLP policy trained on $300$k environment frames; a
richer policy class or a longer training budget would be expected to
close it further.

\subsection{Perturbation-robust reweighting}
\label{sec:sim-m3b}
To probe robustness we evaluate two policies under i.i.d.\ Bernoulli
edge failures at rates $p \in \{0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3\}$: (i) the
clean-trained PPO policy from Section~\ref{sec:sim-m2}, and (ii) a
PPO policy trained with perturbation-in-the-loop at $p = 0.15$.
Figure~\ref{fig:m3b} overlays the $\lambda_2$ curves for both
variants against uniform and Metropolis.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{m3b_robustness_clean.pdf}
\caption{$\lambda_2$ vs.\ edge-failure probability for the
clean-trained PPO policy and classical baselines, averaged over 20
perturbation draws per point. PPO dominates both uniform and
Metropolis at every failure rate.}
\label{fig:m3b}
\end{figure}

At $p = 0$ the clean-trained PPO reaches $\lambda_2 = 0.607$ versus
uniform $0.329$ and Metropolis $0.115$. At $p = 0.1$ the lead grows:
PPO $0.462$, uniform $0.233$, Metropolis $0.079$. At the most severe
rate we tested, $p = 0.3$, the PPO policy still attains $0.158$
versus uniform $0.091$ and Metropolis $0.028$. The
perturbation-trained variant pays a small clean-regime tax ($0.548$
vs.\ $0.607$ at $p{=}0$) but closes to within $10\%$ of the
clean-trained policy by $p{=}0.1$ and tracks it thereafter, as
expected for a policy that was never shown clean rollouts during
training. The headline is that robustness, under the failure-rate
range tested here, is achieved primarily by the spectral-gap
objective itself: a policy that concentrates weight on a few
spectrally important edges happens to degrade gracefully under i.i.d.\
edge drops.

\subsection{Geometric swarm control}
\label{sec:sim-m3c}
In the geometric extension, $n = 20$ agents inhabit the unit square
and edges are weighted by a truncated Gaussian kernel
$W_{ij} = \exp(-(\|p_i - p_j\|/r)^2)$ with $r = 0.15$ and cutoff
$3r$. The action is a per-agent planar velocity $v_i$ with
$\|v_i\| \le v_{\max} = 0.02$. We compare PPO against three
baselines over 10 evaluation seeds: (i) \emph{random} velocities,
(ii) an \emph{unconstrained centroid} heuristic that pulls every
agent toward the group centroid, and (iii) a \emph{constrained
centroid} that freezes an agent once its minimum pairwise distance
falls below the collision radius $r_{\min} = 0.08$. The results are
summarized in Table~\ref{tab:m3c}, and Figure~\ref{fig:m3c-curves}
plots the episode-level $\lambda_2$ traces.

10 geometric-swarm seeds. PPO builds spectral gap while preserving
inter-agent spacing; the unconstrained centroid spikes to
$\lambda_2 \approx 19.8$ by collapsing agents onto a single point.}
\label{fig:m3c-curves}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Geometric swarm evaluation over 10 seeds, mean values.
\emph{mean\_min\_dist} is the minimum pairwise inter-agent distance
averaged across agents and time; the collision radius is $r_{\min} = 0.08$.}
\label{tab:m3c}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Policy & $\lambda_2$ (final) & mean\_min\_dist \\
\midrule
PPO                      & $0.34$  & $0.034$ \\

The unconstrained-centroid $\lambda_2 \approx 19.83$ is a degenerate
optimum: it is achieved by collapsing all agents to a single point,
at which the Gaussian-kernel Laplacian converges to a near-complete
graph with $\lambda_2 \to n = 20$. The accompanying mean minimum
pairwise distance is $0.003$---about $25\times$ below the collision
radius $r_{\min}$. Because any physical realization of the swarm
would be infeasible at that density, this baseline is reported for
completeness but is not a fair comparator. Against the fair
collision-respecting comparator, PPO attains a mean final $\lambda_2$
of $0.34$ versus $0.30$ for the constrained centroid and $0.27$ for
random velocities, and wins in $6$ out of $10$ seeds. PPO spreads
agents toward a configuration that keeps minimum pairwise distance
comparable to the constrained centroid while achieving a larger
spectral gap, which is the qualitative behavior one would hope for
from a position-controlled consensus swarm.

\subsection{Discrete rewiring: a negative result}
\label{sec:sim-m3a}
We finally report the discrete-rewiring variant, in which the
continuous weight action is replaced by a categorical action over
edge toggles. On an Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi support with $n = 20$, the
action space has $\binom{n}{2} = 190$ pairs, and the policy selects
one pair per step to add or remove subject to connectivity and
edge-budget constraints. We compare PPO against a random rewiring
baseline and a greedy swap oracle over 10 evaluation seeds disjoint
from training.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{m3a_lambda2_final_bar.pdf}
\caption{Final-episode $\lambda_2$ by rewiring policy on 10 held-out
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi supports. PPO with a flat MLP categorical head
fails to generalize across topologies and is outperformed by random
rewiring on every seed.}
\label{fig:m3a}
\end{figure}

The result, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:m3a}, is that PPO is
outperformed by random rewiring on \emph{every one} of the 10 held-out
seeds, with the greedy oracle roughly a factor of $3$--$4$ above PPO.
Diagnostic analysis suggests a clean architectural cause: a flat MLP
over a 190-way categorical action space has no mechanism to
generalize across topologies, because the action index of a given
edge pair $(i, j)$ has no stable semantic meaning when the sampled
support changes between episodes. Under i.i.d.\ Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi
resampling, the same logit position corresponds to an
existing edge in one episode and a non-existing one in the next, so
the policy cannot learn a transferable ``which edge to toggle''
heuristic. This is consistent with the broader graph-reinforcement-
learning literature, where permutation-equivariant graph-neural-
network actors are used precisely to make the policy behave
identically under relabelings of the node
set~\cite{KipfWelling2017,Dai2017,Darvariu2021,Darvariu2024}. We
therefore report this as a negative architectural result and defer
the equivariant-policy design to future work (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion},
item~1).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have introduced a convex-relaxation view of policy-learned
algebraic-connectivity maximization: the FDLA SDP of Boyd and Ghosh
provides a tight, polynomial-time-computable upper bound
(Theorem~\ref{thm:fdla}) against which any reweighting policy can be
benchmarked via the suboptimality ratio
$\rho_\pi = \lambda_2(L(w_\pi))/s^\star$. A PPO policy on
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi supports attains $\rho_\pi \approx 0.87$ and a
mean $1.31\times$ lift over uniform baselines, continues to dominate
classical baselines under stochastic edge failures, and in a
geometric swarm regime beats a collision-respecting centroid
heuristic while preserving inter-agent spacing. A discrete-rewiring
variant based on a flat MLP categorical head fails to generalize
across topologies, a clean diagnostic motivating the first item of
future work.

Five directions naturally follow. (1) \emph{Permutation-equivariant
actors.} Replacing the flat MLP with a graph neural
network~\cite{KipfWelling2017,Dai2017,Darvariu2021} should address
the generalization failure observed in
Section~\ref{sec:sim-m3a}. (2) \emph{Decentralized observations.}
Replacing the global-Laplacian spectrum in Definition~\ref{def:mdp}
with a distributed Fiedler-vector
estimate~\cite{YangFreeman2010,SabattiniEtAl2013} would address the
modeling scope limitation of Remark~\ref{rem:scope} and make the
policy implementable in a truly distributed setting. (3)
\emph{Time-varying supports.} Lifting the fixed-support assumption of
Theorem~\ref{thm:fdla} toward jump-Markov or
continually-drifting graph families would test the learning approach
in a regime where retraining the SDP every step is infeasible.
(4) \emph{Rate guarantees.} Translating the empirical
$\rho_\pi$ into a finite-sample high-probability bound, perhaps via
the tools of~\cite{Olshevsky2009}, would give the learned policy a
convergence certificate rather than a benchmark. (5)
\emph{Hardware-in-the-loop validation.} Instantiating the geometric
variant on a physical multi-robot testbed would close the loop
between spectral-gap optimization and the swarm-robotics motivation
that inspired this work.
